🌟 pitting A against B /ˈpɪtɪŋ eɪ əˈɡɛnst biː/

Part of speech: Idiomatic expression (verb phrase)

📖 Meaning:

To pit A against B means to set two people, ideas, groups, or things in direct opposition, conflict, or competition with each other.

Example: The debate pitted science against religion. (= The two were positioned as competing or conflicting sides.)

🧩 Synonyms /ˈsɪnəˌnɪmz/

  • Oppose
  • Confront
  • Challenge
  • Set against
  • Compete
  • Contrast

🔍 Etymology of the Phrase /ˌɛtəˈmɑːlədʒi/

📚 The verb pit comes from the noun pit — like a fighting pit — where opponents face each other. To “pit someone against” another comes from the idea of throwing two fighters into a pit to battle.

💡 Today, it's used more broadly: debates, ideas, sports teams, even inner emotions can be pitted against one another.

🧭 Word Evolution

Old English: pytt (a hole or pit)
⬇️
Middle English: pitten (to throw into a pit)
⬇️
Modern English: to pit A against B = to place in conflict or opposition

📊 Comparison

Phrase Used With Meaning Example
Oppose Ideas, people, actions To resist or go against He opposed the new law.
Compete People or groups To try to win or do better The teams competed for the trophy.
Pit A against B Any two forces To set up a rivalry or comparison The book pits logic against emotion.
