🌟 ditch /dɪtʃ/

Part of speech: Noun or verb

📖 As a verb (action):

To ditch someone or something means to abandon them suddenly or to get rid of something that is no longer wanted or needed.

Example: She ditched her old phone and bought the latest model.

📖 As a noun (a thing):

A ditch is a long, narrow hole in the ground used to carry water or mark the edge of land.

Example: The car slid off the road and into a ditch.

🧩 Synonyms /ˈsɪnəˌnɪmz/

  • Abandon
  • Leave behind
  • Discard
  • Escape from
  • Trench (noun)
  • Channel (noun)

🔍 Etymology of the word /ˌɛtəˈmɑːlədʒi/

📚 Let’s look at the origin (etymology) of the word "ditch".

📝It comes from the Old English word “dīc”, which referred to a trench or moat. The same root gave us the word dyke.

  • dīc (Old English) = trench, moat

✍️ Originally, the verb "ditch" meant to dig a ditch or make a trench, and over time it took on figurative meanings like abandoning or throwing away.

💡 The shift from literal to informal usage reflects how we use physical metaphors to describe emotional or social actions, like “ditching” a plan or person.
