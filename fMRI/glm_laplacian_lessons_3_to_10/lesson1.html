Standard First-Level GLM: \(y=X\beta+\varepsilon\)

This page explains the most important equation in the project using one surface vertex: measured BOLD signal = predicted task signals × beta weights + leftover noise.

1. The measured BOLD signal \(y\)

This is the thing we want to explain. It goes up and down over time.

Measured BOLD signal at one vertex
Zero-abstraction meaning: \(y\) is just a list of 128 measured numbers from one vertex.

2. The design matrix \(X\)

The design matrix is a spreadsheet of predicted signals. In the real project it has 128 rows and 18 columns.

\[ \underbrace{X}_{128\times 18} = \begin{bmatrix} \text{row 1 predictions}\\ \text{row 2 predictions}\\ \vdots\\ \text{row 128 predictions} \end{bmatrix} \]
Three example columns from \(X\)
Each column says: “If this condition explains the brain signal, the timecourse should look like this.”

3. The beta vector \(eta\)

The GLM chooses one weight for each design-matrix column.

\[ \underbrace{\beta}_{18\times 1} = \begin{bmatrix} \beta_1\\\beta_2\\\vdots\\\beta_{18} \end{bmatrix} \]

5. The leftover noise \( arepsilon\)

The model is never perfect. The difference between measured and predicted is the residual/noise.

\[ \varepsilon = y-X\beta \]
