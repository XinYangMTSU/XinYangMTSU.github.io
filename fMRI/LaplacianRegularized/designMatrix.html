What is the Design Matrix in this Nilearn fMRI project?

We build the design matrix as a spreadsheet with 128 rows and 18 columns. Every row is one scan time. Every column is one “fake signal” the GLM uses to explain the real BOLD signal.

0. The whole idea in one sentence

The design matrix is not the brain data. It is a table of predicted BOLD time courses created from the experiment schedule.

128
rows
One row for each fMRI volume/time point.
18
columns
Sixteen task-related predictions plus two slow drift predictions.

Why not stop at the 0/1 column?
Because the BOLD signal is slow. The brain does not jump instantly at the event row; the response rises and falls over several scans.

3. Why each condition gives two columns

For each task condition, Nilearn creates a main HRF column and a derivative column.

Main HRF column
“If this task activates this vertex, the BOLD signal should follow this delayed bump.”
Derivative column
“Maybe the bump is a little earlier or later. Let the model adjust timing slightly.”

6. How GLM uses this table

At one surface vertex, the measured BOLD signal is another 128-value column. The model asks: what mixture of the 18 design columns best reconstructs this measured signal?

\[ \text{actual BOLD signal} \approx \beta_1 \cdot \text{column 1} + \beta_2 \cdot \text{column 2} + \cdots + \beta_{18} \cdot \text{column 18} \]
Example: one fake measured BOLD signal over all 128 points
Plain-English translation: The design matrix gives the model 18 candidate explanations. The GLM chooses 18 weights, called betas, to combine those explanations.
