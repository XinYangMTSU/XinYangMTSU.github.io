Where Every Great Discovery Begins with a Question
Book One
Chapter One
The Drive Home
"Every great discovery begins with a question." — The First Rule of Infinity Academy

Ada was already waiting when Mom’s car reached the school pickup line.

She stood near the sidewalk with her backpack hanging from one shoulder and a book pressed against her chest. As soon as she recognized the car, she smiled, hurried forward, and climbed into the back seat.

“Hi, Mom.”

“Hi, Ada. How was school?”

“Good.”

Mom pulled away from the curb. She knew that good was rarely Ada’s complete answer. Usually, another part arrived after they had driven a little farther.

Sure enough, before they reached the first traffic light, Ada said,

“We learned about factors today.”

“Oh?”

“A factor is a number that can divide another number without anything left over.”

“That sounds right.”

Ada looked through the window as houses, trees, and mailboxes passed by.

“Mom?”

“Yes?”

“Why do some numbers have lots of factors, but some numbers hardly have any?”

Mom glanced at her in the rearview mirror.

“That is a very good question.”

Ada waited.

Mom did not continue.

“Well?” Ada asked.

“Well what?”

“What is the answer?”

“I’m not sure I can give you the whole answer while I’m driving.”

“But you know it, right?”

“I know part of it,” Mom said. “But sometimes knowing part of an answer gives you an even bigger question.”

Ada considered this.

She liked answers, but she liked questions too—especially questions that made ordinary things seem less ordinary.

At the next red light, she opened the book she had been carrying. It was Harry Potter and the Sorcerer's Stone. She had already watched the movie twice and listened to the story once, but reading the original book felt different.

In the book, she could stop wherever she wanted. She could reread a sentence, imagine the Great Hall in her own way, and remain inside the story for as long as she pleased.

“Wouldn't it be wonderful,” she said, “if Hogwarts were a real school?”

“It might be,” Mom replied.

“You could study spells instead of regular subjects.”

“Would you still practice piano?”

Ada looked doubtful.

“Maybe Hogwarts has a piano spell.”

“A spell that practices for you?”

“Yes.”

“That sounds dangerous.”

“Why?”

“Because then the spell would become a better pianist than you.”

Ada laughed.

“Spells can't become pianists.”

“How do you know?”

Ada opened her mouth, then paused.

Mom smiled.

“Another question.”

When they arrived home, Ada carried her backpack inside, but the book never made it to the bookshelf. It landed on the couch in the playroom, beside two drawing books, a library book about animals, and another novel she had started the week before.

Books were everywhere in their house.

Some stood neatly on the shelves in the reading room. Others formed small piles on the floor, rested beside the piano, leaned against the couch, or waited on tables with bookmarks sticking from their pages.

Mom sometimes asked Ada to put them away. Ada usually did—although somehow, by the next evening, the books had spread out again.

After changing her clothes, Ada looked outside.

The afternoon was bright and mild. Her bicycle waited near the garage, and her roller skates were beside it.

“Bike or skates?” Mom asked.

Ada examined both choices as seriously as if she were deciding between two secret passages.

“Bike first.”

She wheeled it onto the driveway and began riding in long loops...

She rode toward the mailbox, turned, passed the garage, and circled back again. On each turn, she leaned slightly to one side.

After several rounds, she stopped near Mom.

“Why do I have to lean when I turn?”

“So you don't fall over.”

“I know. But why does leaning stop me from falling?”

Mom thought for a moment.

“That question may belong to physics.”

“Is physics math?”

“Physics uses a lot of mathematics.”

“So math can explain bicycle riding?”

“It can explain part of it.”

Ada looked down at the wheels.

The bicycle had seemed perfectly ordinary a moment earlier. Now it appeared to contain a secret.

She began riding again, making tighter and tighter circles just to notice what her body did.

Later, she exchanged the bicycle for her roller skates. The wheels clicked against the driveway as she moved forward, turned, and practiced stopping.

Ballet had taught her to think about posture. Gymnastics had taught her to notice balance. Ice skating had made her pay attention to edges and turns. Tennis had made her watch where a ball was going before it arrived.

But she had never thought that mathematics might be hiding inside those things.

By the time she came indoors, Mom had already reminded her twice about piano practice.

Ada sat at the piano and opened her music.

She played the first few measures, stopped, and tried again. One passage did not sound right. She played it slowly, then even more slowly, counting the beats aloud.

“One, two, three, four. One, two, three, four.”

She paused.

“Mom?”

“Yes?”

“Why does music need counting?”

“So everyone knows when the notes should happen.”

“But music doesn't sound like counting.”

“No,” Mom said. “It sounds like music.”

Ada played the passage once more.

This time, the notes fell into place.

She looked at the page. The music was made of black symbols and carefully divided measures. Somehow, when she followed their pattern, the piano produced something beautiful.

That seemed a little like magic.

After dinner, Ada returned to the playroom and curled up on the couch beneath the large reading lamp. Its warm light spread over the pages of Harry Potter while the rest of the room remained softly shadowed.

She read until she reached the part where Harry began discovering that the world he had always known was much larger and stranger than he had imagined.

Ada lowered the book.

“Mom?”

Mom was nearby, gathering a few of the books that had collected on the floor.

“Yes?”

“Do you think there could really be a secret school somewhere?”

“A school like Hogwarts?”

“Not exactly.”

“What kind of school?”

Ada looked around the reading room.

There were books about animals, stories, history, science, art, China, music, space, and places their family hoped to visit one day. Some of the books were in English. A few were in Chinese, a language Ada could speak but could not yet read.

“A school where everything is connected,” she said.

Mom stopped gathering books.

“What do you mean?”

“Like my bicycle and math. And piano and counting. And skating and circles. Maybe they are connected, but people don't notice.”

Mom sat beside her.

“That sounds like a school for exploration.”

Ada nodded.

“A school where you don't just memorize answers.”

“What would students do instead?”

“They would ask questions.”

“And what would they study?”

Ada thought about the factors from school.

“Numbers.”

She thought about turning her bicycle and spinning on skates.

“Shapes and circles.”

She remembered the rhythm of her piano music.

“Patterns.”

“What else?”

“Puzzles. Probability. Maybe time.”

She became more excited as she spoke.

“And there could be creatures made from numbers. And doors that only open when you solve something.”

Mom smiled.

“That sounds like a wonderful school.”

“What would it be called?”

They sat quietly beneath the reading lamp.

Outside, the driveway was empty now. Ada's bicycle and roller skates had been put away. Inside, the piano stood silent, and books covered nearly every available surface.

Mom looked at Ada.

“What is the largest number you can imagine?”

“A billion.”

“Can you add one to it?”

“Yes.”

“Then is it really the largest?”

Ada frowned.

“No.”

“What if you imagined a number much larger than a billion?”

“I could still add one.”

“So where do numbers end?”

Ada stared at her mother.

“They don't.”

“And what do we call something that never ends?”

Ada knew this word.

“Infinity.”

Mom nodded.

Ada looked down at the open book in her lap. Then she looked at the shelves, the piano, the drawing paper, and the pool of golden light surrounding the couch.

“Infinity Academy,” she whispered.

The name seemed to settle into the room as though it had been waiting there all along.

Ada smiled.

“Can we create it?”

Mom placed the book she was holding on the table.

“Yes,” she said. “I think we already have.”

Ada looked around again, but nothing had changed. The walls were the same. The books were still everywhere. The reading lamp shone exactly as it had before.

Yet the room no longer felt quite ordinary.

“Where do we start?” Ada asked.

Mom picked up a pencil and gave it to her.

“With a question.”

Ada opened her drawing notebook to a clean page.

At the top, she carefully wrote:

INFINITY ACADEMY

Beneath it, she added:

Every great discovery begins with a question.

Then she paused, holding the pencil above the paper.

At last, she wrote the first question:

Why are some numbers different from all the others?

Neither Ada nor Mom noticed that, somewhere among the books behind them, a page quietly turned by itself.
